Reliability analysis of the adapted version of the SISCO Inventory of Academic Stress by Barraza (2007)
The reliability of the adapted version of Barraza’s (2007) SISCO Inventory of Academic Stress was assessed using Cronbach’s Alpha, yielding a value of 0.897. This indicates a high internal consistency among the 31 items of the questionnaire. The KMO index was 0.900, and Bartlett’s test of sphericity showed a Chi-Squared value of 3047.465 (465 df, p < 0.000), indicating significant correlations between the variables. A five-component structure was chosen, aligned with the structure proposed by Barraza (2007), explaining 50.95% of the total variance.
Although the explained variance of the questionnaire was 50.95%, which may be considered somewhat low in specific contexts, this variance is acceptable in studies investigating complex psychological constructs like academic stress. Stress is influenced by numerous factors, many of which may not be fully captured by a single instrument. In line with previous studies on stress and psychological well-being (Chen et al., 2024; Broks et al., 2024), variances of around 50% are common when dealing with multidimensional constructs, as internal and external variables shape these. Thus, while higher variance would be ideal, the results still provide valuable insights into how the measured factors contribute to academic stress.

The analysis of the questionnaire administered to the students of the Faculty of Education of the ULL revealed significant aspects of academic stress in several dimensions. The study found that the main situations associated with academic stress, with an overall mean of 3.42, included task and assignment overload (= 4.08) and faculty assessments (= 3.96). In contrast to these stressors, peer competitiveness (= 2.43) and class participation (= 2.96) generated less stress. They had an average score of 3.04 regarding physical reactions to academic stress; chronic fatigue (= 3.35) and drowsiness (= 3.33) were identified as the most common. On the other hand, reactions such as body itching (= 2.65) and digestive problems (= 2.77) occurred to a lesser extent.
Psychological reactions also displayed an interesting pattern, with an average score of 3.14. Students primarily experienced difficulties in concentration (= 3.50) and feelings of anxiety or despair (= 3.35), while reactions of irritability or aggressiveness (= 2.53) and feelings of depression and sadness (= 2.98) were less common. Regarding behavioral reactions, with an average score of 2.78, a lack of motivation for academic tasks (= 3.45) and increased food consumption (= 3.10) emerged as common responses to stress. Less frequent reactions included reduced food consumption (= 2.17) and increased conflicts and arguments (= 2.54). Finally, academic stress coping strategies, with an average score of 2.65, were observed to be the most common practice by students, including developing a plan (= 3.50) and using assertiveness (= 3.32). In contrast, religiosity (= 1.64) and seeking professional help (= 1.85) were less frequently employed strategies.

Correlation analysis between academic stress and academic performance A Pearson correlation analysis was conducted further to explore the relationship between academic stress and academic performance. The results revealed a weak negative correlation between students’ overall academic stress levels and academic performance (r = −0.22, p < 0.05). This suggests that as academic stress increases, academic performance slightly decreases. However, this relationship was not strong enough to imply a direct predictive effect. These findings align with prior research (Chen et al., 2024; Kristensen et al., 2023), indicating that while stress may impact performance, other factors—such as coping strategies and institutional support—can mitigate its effects.

They highlighted an excess of educational tasks, ambiguous continuous assessment, traditional teaching methods, self-demand, maintaining scholarships, and the elimination of the September examination session. Regarding consequences, students reported severe physical reactions (insomnia, digestive issues, requiring doctor appointments) and psychological effects (anxiety, feeling "in a cloud"). To cope with stress, strategies included disconnecting the day before exams, doing enjoyable activities instead of last-minute studying, organizing material in advance, listening to ASMR, and seeking professional psychological help. Measures proposed by students to reduce stress included making continuous assessment more realistic, reintroducing September recovery exams, receiving timely teacher feedback, and designing practical study plans. Students also suggested the university provide study technique advisors and memorization training, which is crucial for low-income students. Finally, opinions on performance influenced by stress were mixed; some perform well under pressure (viewing it as a "sword of Damocles"), while others underperform significantly due to intense self-demand. In addition to the qualitative analysis, descriptive statistics showed that 80% of participants mentioned stress related to academic workload, while 60% cited exam-related stress.
Discussion This study comprehensively explores the academic stress experienced by students in the Faculty of Education at ULL. The findings align with previous literature, highlighting academic stress’s complexity. One key insight is that academic stress can positively and negatively affect performance. While acute stress can act as a motivator, chronic stress undermines health. This study found no significant correlation between overall stress levels and grades, challenging the assumption that higher stress necessarily leads to lower academic performance. This suggests that time management and adaptive coping strategies moderate the effects of stress (Mize, 2024). Another important finding is that stress is not confined to exam periods; it persists throughout the academic year due to continuous assessment models involving an ongoing workload. The impact on physical and psychological health is significant, with students reporting sleep disorders, fatigue, anxiety, and demotivation. Students primarily relied on planning and organizing course materials to manage stress, yet they underutilized psychological support services. Although no significant differences were found between undergraduate and postgraduate students, this suggests common stressors transcend academic levels. One limitation is the small number of participants in the focus group (four students), which may limit the generalizability of the qualitative findings.
Conclusions This study illuminated the complex phenomenon of academic stress. Stressors vary from task overload to concerns about family conciliation and scholarships. The continuous assessment modality appears to be a significant source of stress. Academic stress significantly impacts physical and psychological health, yet it does not show a strong correlation with lower grades, suggesting coping strategies are highly influential. Females appear more prone to experiencing academic stress than males. Students rely heavily on planning, but underutilize psychological resources. These findings have important implications for educational institutions, which should implement psychological support programs, review assessment practices to ensure a fair workload distribution, and promote healthy coping strategies. Expanding the sample size and incorporating variables like socioeconomic status in future research will help develop more effective strategies to improve student well-being in higher education.
